Module 01 Foundation

The Foundation: Deep Relaxation & Body Awareness

Learn to relax the body completely while staying mentally present — the physical platform every other technique is built on.

By the end you can

  • Perform a full head-to-toe progressive muscle relaxation pass and reach physical stillness on demand, letting urges to move pass un-serviced.
  • Use slow diaphragmatic breathing with a long exhale to down-regulate arousal — and explain why the long out-breath calms you.
  • Notice and name subtle body sensations — heaviness, tingling, warmth, the "fading" of a limb — without reacting, holding a flat, curious observer stance.
  • Hold a comfortable projection-ready posture for 15–20 minutes, awake and present rather than simply falling asleep.

Prerequisites: 00 · Orientation

✦ How to read this module

You have already (in Orientation) set up a practice space, started a journal, and read the safety boundaries. This module assumes nothing beyond that. We do not touch the threshold state, the vibrational state, or any exit technique here — those come in Modules 02–05. Your one job here is to build a body that will lie still, soft, and quiet on demand, and a mind that stays awake to watch it. Master this and everything later becomes possible. Skip it and everything later becomes frustrating.

Lesson 1

Why relaxation is the platform

Every account of astral projection — Robert Monroe's, William Buhlman's, Robert Bruce's — starts in the same unglamorous place: a body so deeply relaxed it almost disappears, while the mind stays clear. The tradition has a tidy slogan for the goal of the next module, "mind awake, body asleep." You can't get there until the body part is reliable. That is what this module trains.

Here is the plain-language logic, no metaphysics required:

  • A tense body keeps the brain on alert. Muscle tension, a racing heart, and quick shallow breathing are the body's "something is happening" signals. As long as they're firing, your nervous system stays in its activated, ready-to-move mode (the sympathetic "fight-or-flight" branch). You cannot drift toward the edge of sleep while your body is shouting stay ready.
  • Deep relaxation flips a real physiological switch. When you systematically release muscle tension and slow your breathing — especially the out-breath — you shift the balance toward the parasympathetic "rest-and-digest" branch. Heart rate settles, arousal drops, attention softens. This is measurable, not mystical evidenced: progressive muscle relaxation produces steady decreases in skin electrical activity (a marker of parasympathetic activation) across a session, and slow breathing with a long exhale reliably raises heart-rate variability, a sign of stronger vagal tone.
  • A relaxed-but-awake body is the launchpad. Once the body is deeply settled and you are still consciously present, you are in the doorway. Modules 02+ teach you how to stand in that doorway without falling through it into ordinary sleep. But the doorway only exists once the body is relaxed enough. No platform, no projection.

The three lenses, kept separate

A habit this whole course holds to: we keep what is experiential, evidenced, and interpretive clearly apart.

★ The tradition holds

interpretive That the heaviness, tingling, warmth, and the felt "fading" of a limb are "energy," the loosening of the astral body, or pre-separation signs. We will name this framing when it's relevant — but in this module we simply observe the sensations and assert no cause.

◎ The grounded view

experiential Practitioners report heaviness, tingling, warmth, a limb "vanishing," the body feeling huge/tiny/numb — constantly; you will likely feel them. evidenced Parasympathetic down-regulation from PMR and slow breathing, the body-scan/interoception research base, and respiratory sinus arrhythmia (the heart slows on the exhale) are measured and studied.

✦ A grounded reassurance you can lean on now

Nothing in this module asks you to leave anything. You are learning to relax and to pay attention. The strange sensations you'll meet (a limb "vanishing," the body feeling like it's tilting) are well-known products of deep relaxation and the brain's body-map quieting down — harmless, reversible the instant you move, and exactly the raw material you'll work with later.

If at any point you want out, you open your eyes, wiggle your fingers and toes, and you're fully back. That escape hatch never closes.

⚠ Safety, woven in

Relaxation practice is gentle and broadly safe. Three honest notes:

  • If Orientation flagged you (history of psychosis or schizophrenia-spectrum conditions, dissociative disorders, severe anxiety/panic), the guidance there still holds — relaxation and body scanning are usually fine and even helpful, but check with a professional before intensive practice, and stop if a session reliably increases distress or a feeling of unreality.
  • A small number of people feel more anxious when they turn attention inward ("relaxation-induced anxiety"). If that's you, keep sessions short, keep your eyes softly open, and favor the breathing and tension-release drills over long silent body scans. It usually eases with practice.
  • This is not medical or psychological treatment and makes no health claims. It's a skill-building practice.

Lesson 2

Progressive Muscle Relaxation: tension, then release

The idea. Developed by physician Edmund Jacobson (who published Progressive Relaxation in 1938), PMR works by a simple trick of contrast: you deliberately tense a muscle group for a few seconds, then let it go all at once and notice the release. Two things make this powerful for a beginner:

  1. Contrast teaches your nervous system what "released" actually feels like. Most people walk around with low-grade tension they can't even detect. Tensing first makes the letting-go unmistakable — you can't miss the difference.
  2. The release itself down-regulates arousal. Across a PMR session, parasympathetic activity rises progressively (measurable as falling skin-conductance), which is precisely the settling you want.

evidenced PMR is one of the better-evidenced relaxation tools generally — it was rated an effective non-drug treatment for chronic insomnia by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. That's relevant here because falling asleep too fast is the beginner's main enemy, and a body this relaxed is one you'll later have to learn to keep on the edge of sleep rather than over it.

The exact tension-and-release rule (memorize this)

  • Tense the target group firmly but never to the point of pain or cramp — about 70% effort, not a maximal strain. Joints and old injuries: go light or skip.
  • Hold the tension for about 5 seconds while you keep breathing (don't hold your breath).
  • Release completely and instantly — don't ease off gradually, drop it — and let that group go limp.
  • Rest and notice the contrast for about 10–15 seconds before moving on. This noticing is the actual training, not the tensing.

The exact muscle-group sequence

Work it head-to-toe so the deepest relaxation pools where you'll rest last (feet-up is also fine and traditional; pick one and be consistent). Here is the standard head-to-toe order, condensed to a beginner-friendly set of groups:

  1. Forehead & scalp — raise your eyebrows high, wrinkle the forehead. Release.
  2. Eyes & cheeks — squeeze your eyes shut tight and scrunch the nose. Release.
  3. Jaw & mouth — clench the jaw and press the lips together (don't grind teeth). Release; let the jaw hang slightly open.
  4. Neck — gently press the back of your head down into the surface, or lightly tip the chin toward the chest. Go easy here. Release.
  5. Shoulders — shrug them up toward your ears. Release; let them melt down and back.
  6. Upper arms — bend the elbows and tense the biceps. Release.
  7. Forearms & hands — make tight fists and tense down to the wrists. Release; let the fingers half-curl open.
  8. Chest & upper back — take a fuller breath and squeeze the chest/shoulder blades gently together. Release as you exhale.
  9. Abdomen — tighten the belly as if bracing for a light poke. Release.
  10. Buttocks & hips — squeeze the glutes together. Release.
  11. Thighs — press the thighs down / tense the quads. Release.
  12. Calves — gently point the toes toward your face (flex the ankle) to tense the calves. (Pointing toes away can cramp — flexing toward you is safer.) Release.
  13. Feet & toes — curl the toes downward lightly. Release.

End by lying still and letting the whole body be heavy for a minute. That's one full PMR pass — roughly 8–12 minutes at an unhurried pace.

⚠ Beginner error, flagged now

Tensing while you think you're relaxing. People clench the jaw, hunch the shoulders, or curl the toes the whole session without noticing. PMR's tense-then-release contrast is the antidote — and below we'll add a "scan for hidden tension" habit.

Lesson 3

The point-by-point body scan

The idea. Where PMR does something to each muscle, the body scan does the opposite: you just pay attention to each part of the body in turn, with no agenda to change it. You move a spotlight of attention slowly from point to point, noticing whatever sensation is there — pressure, warmth, tingling, weight, or nothing at all — and then moving on.

This is the single most important skill in the whole module, because noticing body sensation without reacting is the core skill of every later stage. The vibrational state (Module 03) and the exit cues (Module 05) are all sensations you observe without grabbing. The body scan is where you build that muscle.

evidenced It's also well-studied: the body scan sits at the heart of Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR), one of the most-researched behavioral interventions there is, and regular practice measurably improves interoception — your accuracy at reading internal body signals — and reduces the tendency to catastrophically misread normal sensations. That last point matters: you're literally training the skill of feeling a weird sensation and not panicking about it.

The route (classic MBSR order, lying down)

Start at the toes of one foot → sole, top, and heel of that foot → ankle → lower leg → knee → thigh → then cross over and do the other leg the same way, foot up to thigh → pelvis and hips → lower back → abdomen → chest → upper back → shoulders → upper arms → forearms → hands and fingers (one arm then the other, or both together) → neck and throat → jaw and face → scalp → and finally rest in awareness of the whole body at once as a single field.

How to actually do it

  • Spend roughly 20–40 seconds per region, longer if it's interesting, shorter if attention won't settle. A full scan runs ~10–20 minutes.
  • Don't try to relax the part — just feel it. Paradoxically, attention-without-agenda is what lets a part release. If it doesn't release, that's fine; note "tense" and move on.
  • When you find no sensation in a region, that is the observation. Note "blank" or "neutral" and move on. (That neutrality is often the doorway to the "fading" feeling below.)
  • When the mind wanders (it will, constantly), note where it went and gently bring the spotlight back to where you left off. The returning is the practice; you're not failing when you wander.

Lesson 4

Slow diaphragmatic breathing

The idea. The breath is the one autonomic process you can steer by hand, and steering it slow — especially a long exhale — is the fastest reliable way to down-regulate arousal.

Get the mechanics right first (diaphragmatic = "belly" breathing)

  • Lie down, one hand on your chest, one on your belly.
  • Breathe in through the nose so that the belly hand rises and the chest hand stays nearly still. You're letting the diaphragm drop and the belly balloon out. Shallow chest breathing (chest hand rising, belly still) is the thing to unlearn — it keeps arousal up.
  • The out-breath is passive and slow — let the belly fall.

Why the long exhale specifically calms you

evidenced Your heart rate naturally speeds slightly on the inhale and slows on the exhale — this is respiratory sinus arrhythmia. On the exhale, the vagus nerve releases acetylcholine and slows the heart; a longer exhale stretches out that parasympathetic, heart-slowing phase. So making the exhale longer than the inhale acutely raises vagal (parasympathetic) tone. That's the whole reason the counts below are weighted toward the out-breath.

Two concrete patterns — pick by feel

(A) Simple slow belly breathing (start here). No breath-holding, gentle, sustainable for the whole session:

(A) SIMPLE SLOW BELLY BREATH  ·  ~6 breaths/min
  IN   through the nose ....... count 4   (belly rises)
  OUT  through nose/lips ...... count 6   (belly falls, slow & smooth)
  → ~10-second cycle. Keep it soft and even.
  → This is your default for long sessions: it never makes you light-headed.

That's near the rate where heart-rate variability and the relaxation response peak.

(B) The 4-7-8 breath (a stronger, shorter down-shift). evidenced Popularized as a relaxation/sleep tool; a scoping review across 15 studies links it to reduced acute stress and increased heart-rate variability via vagal pathways (note: promising but still needing more independent replication — that's the honest evidence status). The pattern, done with the tongue on the ridge behind the upper teeth as in the original:

(B) THE 4-7-8 BREATH  ·  3–4 cycles ONLY
  INHALE  quietly through the nose ........ count 4
  HOLD    relaxed, don't strain ........... count 7
  EXHALE  through the mouth, slow ......... count 8   ← the active ingredient
  → Do only 3–4 cycles, then return to normal breathing.
  → The 7-hold can make beginners light-headed or slightly anxious.
    If so, drop straight back to pattern (A). Don't grind out many rounds.

✦ Important transition note for this practice

You'll use breathing to settle in at the start of a session, but you won't white-knuckle a strict count for 20 minutes. After the first few minutes you let the count dissolve into easy, slow, natural belly breathing so it doesn't keep your mind too busy to settle. (Why this matters becomes obvious in Module 02, where an over-controlled mind won't drift to the threshold.)

Lesson 5

Noticing and naming subtle sensations, without reacting

As the body settles, it starts to report things. The classic menu:

  • Heaviness — limbs feel weighted, sinking, hard to move (often the first sign you're going deep).
  • Tingling / pins-and-needles / a fine buzzing — especially in hands, feet, lips, scalp.
  • Warmth (sometimes coolness) — spreading through the hands, belly, or whole body.
  • Limb "fading" — you lose the sense of where your hand or foot is; it feels absent, dissolved, or like it's not yours. The body can feel oddly large, small, tilted, or like it's gently floating or sinking.

◎ The grounded view (so these never scare you)

evidenced As deep relaxation quiets the steady stream of touch and position signals the brain normally uses to map the body, the map gets fuzzy. A part you're not actively moving and can no longer feel clearly simply drops off the map — hence "fading." None of it means anything is wrong; it means relaxation is working and your body-map is going quiet.

★ The tradition holds

interpretive Some traditions read these sensations as pre-separation signs — the astral body loosening from the physical. For now we make no claim about cause: we just notice.

The one skill to drill: notice → name → don't react → return

  1. Notice the sensation.
  2. Name it, silently and flatly: "heaviness," "tingling in the left hand," "warmth," "the foot's gone fuzzy." Naming keeps you the calm observer instead of getting swept into the feeling.
  3. Don't react. Don't move to check the limb is still there. Don't get excited ("is this it?!"). Don't get scared ("why can't I feel my arm?"). Excitement and fear both spike arousal and yank you back out of the settled state — this is the lesson that pays off hugest later.
  4. Return your attention to the breath or the scan and let the sensation do whatever it does.

That neutral, slightly-curious, non-grabbing stance is the entire emotional skill of this practice. Practice it on a tingle now; you'll need it on much stranger things later.

Lesson 6

The projection-ready posture

This is not your normal sleeping position, and the difference is deliberate. A sleep posture is engineered to drop you unconscious. The projection-ready posture is engineered to let the body go fully relaxed while keeping a thread of consciousness alive — relaxed body, awake mind. (You won't use that awake-mind balance until Module 02; here you're just building the posture so it becomes automatic.)

The default: supine and neutral

  • On your back (supine), lying on a firm-ish surface — a bed is okay but a too-soft mattress that swallows you invites sleep. A yoga mat with a thin pad, or a firm couch, works well.
  • Spine neutral and straight, legs uncrossed and slightly apart (about hip width) so the hips fall open and nothing presses limb-on-limb.
  • Arms at your sides, slightly away from the torso, palms down or up — whatever lets the shoulders drop. Not folded on the chest (restricts the belly breath) and not interlaced (limbs touching keeps the body-map "louder," which fights the fading you want).
  • Head: here's the key anti-sleep tweak. Use a thin pillow or a slight elevation so the head and neck are supported and the chin is very slightly tucked — comfortable, but not the deep, fully-supported sink of your sleeping pillow. Some practitioners deliberately keep the head a touch more upright than for sleep (a low wedge, or practicing semi-reclined in a supported chair at roughly a 40-degree angle) precisely because lying flat the way you sleep is the fast track to unconsciousness.
  • Eyes closed, or — if you fall asleep easily — softly open or half-open, gaze unfocused toward the ceiling. Open eyes is a legitimate, traditional anti-sleep trick.
  • Cool rather than warm, lightly covered rather than cozy. Warmth and heavy blankets are sleep cues. A slightly cool room with a light cover keeps you on the awake side.

Why it differs from a sleep posture — the principle: every choice trades a little bit of "cozy" for a little bit of "stay aware." Firmer surface, thinner pillow, cooler room, head slightly raised, arms not tucked in — each is a small wakefulness anchor. You want the body to believe it's bedtime while your mind keeps one foot in the room. That's the whole craft of the posture.

⚠ Contraindication note

If lying flat on your back is uncomfortable for you (pregnancy in later stages, certain back conditions, reflux), use the semi-reclined supported-chair version instead — it serves the same purpose and is actually better at preventing sleep.

Lesson 7

Holding it for 15–20 minutes

The objective is to lie in the projection-ready posture, deeply relaxed, physically still and mentally present, for 15–20 minutes at a stretch. Two sub-skills:

Physical stillness on demand. Once you've settled, you stop all voluntary movement — no scratching the itch, no shifting the shoulder, no swallowing-on-purpose. This matters because every fidget reloads the body-map (the opposite of fading) and re-engages the alert, ready-to-move system. The trick: resolve, before you start, to be still, and treat every urge to move as just another sensation to notice → name → not react to → let pass. Almost every itch and urge fades within 20–40 seconds if you don't service it. (Genuine pain or pins-and-needles that hurts is the exception — adjust, don't suffer.)

Staying present for the duration. Hold an easy thread of awareness — the slow breath, or a slow repeating scan — so you neither drop into sleep nor get bored and quit. Build the duration gradually (see exercises): 5 minutes solidly still and present is a real win before you chase 20.

✦ A note you'll appreciate later

In this module, if you do fall asleep, that's not a disaster — it means you relaxed thoroughly, which is half the skill. The other half (staying awake at the edge) is literally Module 02's entire job. So train relaxation hard here; we'll add the awake-at-the-edge control next.

Practice it now — the guided session player

This relaxation preset paces your breath with a breathing orb and walks you down through settling, release, the body scan, and rest at the edge. It directly drives this module's practice — run it lying in the projection-ready posture.

Worked examples

Full followable scripts

These are written out so you can read them slowly into a voice memo and play them back, or have someone read them to you, or just internalize the arc. Counts are in seconds. (Optional but recommended: record Script A in your own calm voice with long pauses — it's the single best way to learn the sequence.)

Worked Example A — the full ~15-minute "PMR-then-Scan" session

Setup: Projection-ready posture (Lesson 6). Phone on Do-Not-Disturb, a soft timer set for 16 minutes face-down so you can't clock-watch.

[0:00 — ARRIVAL · ~1 min]
Settle onto your back. Legs hip-width, arms a little away from your
sides, palms up. Let the surface take your full weight. Close your eyes,
or leave them softly open toward the ceiling. Make a quiet resolution:
"for the next fifteen minutes I will lie still, and I will stay here
watching."

[~1:00 — SETTLING BREATH · ~2 min]
Bring one hand's worth of attention to your belly. In through the nose
for a slow count of FOUR — feel the belly rise. Out through the nose for
a slow count of SIX — feel the belly fall.  (in 2-3-4 ... out 2-3-4-5-6)
Again. And again — about a dozen of these slow breaths. Let each exhale
be the longer, heavier one; let it carry a little tension out of you.
After a dozen, stop counting and let the breath run slow on its own.

[~3:00 — PMR PASS · ~7 min]   tense ~70%, never to pain · hold 5 · drop · feel for 10
  Forehead & scalp — lift the eyebrows high ... hold 2-3-4-5 ... RELEASE.
  Eyes & cheeks    — squeeze shut, scrunch the nose ... hold ... RELEASE.
  Jaw             — clench gently, lips together ... hold ... RELEASE (let it hang).
  Neck            — press the head lightly back, go easy ... hold ... RELEASE.
  Shoulders       — shrug up to your ears ... hold ... RELEASE (melt down).
  Upper arms      — bend elbows, tense biceps ... hold ... RELEASE.
  Hands & forearms— tight fists ... hold ... RELEASE (fingers fall open).
  Chest & back    — fuller breath, squeeze the blades ... hold ... RELEASE on the out-breath.
  Belly           — tighten as if braced ... hold ... RELEASE.
  Buttocks & hips — squeeze ... hold ... RELEASE.
  Thighs          — press them down ... hold ... RELEASE.
  Calves          — flex the toes up toward your face ... hold ... RELEASE.
  Feet & toes     — curl the toes down lightly ... hold ... RELEASE.
Now lie completely still and let the whole body be heavy, sinking, for a
slow count of fifteen.

[~10:00 — BODY SCAN · ~5 min]   now we only watch — change nothing
Spotlight on the TOES of the LEFT foot. Feel whatever's there — warmth,
tingling, pressure, or nothing. Name it: "tingling," "warm," "blank."
  ... sole & top of the foot ... ankle ... lower leg ... knee ... thigh.
Cross to the RIGHT toes ... foot ... ankle ... lower leg ... knee ... thigh.
  ... hips & pelvis (heavy) ... lower back ... belly (rising, falling) ...
  chest ... upper back ... shoulders (soft, dropped) ... down the arms ...
  hands & fingers (they may tingle or feel far away — just name it) ...
  neck & throat ... jaw & face (soft) ... scalp.
Now let the whole body be one single field of sensation at once.
Heavy. Quiet. Present.

[~15:00 — REST · until the timer]
Stay here. Slow breath, still body. If a limb has gone fuzzy or vanished,
just name it — "the left hand's faded" — and let it be. You're not going
anywhere; you're resting awake in a very relaxed body. When the timer
sounds: wiggle fingers and toes, take a fuller breath, stretch, and come
all the way back before you stand.

Worked Example B — the 5-minute breathing drill walk-through

Goal: train the diaphragmatic mechanics and the calming long exhale on their own, so they're automatic when you stack them. Do this seated or supine.

PHASE 1 — FIND THE BELLY BREATH  (~1 min)
  One hand on the chest, one on the belly. Breathe normally and just
  WATCH which hand moves. If it's the chest hand, slow down and send the
  next breath low — aim it at the belly hand. Keep going until the belly
  hand rises and the chest hand is nearly still. A relaxed medium breath
  that goes LOW is the target — don't force a big breath.

PHASE 2 — SLOW 4-OUT-6  (~2.5 min, ~15 breaths)
  Add the count.  IN through the nose, belly rising, 2-3-4.
  OUT, belly falling, slow and smooth, 2-3-4-5-6.
  Keep the exhale longer and softer than the inhale — that long exhale
  is the part that slows your heart. If you get light-headed, the breaths
  are too big: make them smaller and slower, not deeper.

PHASE 3 — THREE 4-7-8 CYCLES  (optional, ~1 min)
  Tongue lightly behind the upper front teeth.
  IN nose 4 · HOLD 7 (relaxed, don't strain) · OUT mouth 8.
  That's one cycle. Do three more — no more. If the 7-hold makes you
  tense or dizzy, skip this phase and stay with 4-out-6: just as valid.

PHASE 4 — LET GO OF THE COUNT  (~30 sec)
  Stop counting. Let the breath run slow and natural on its own and
  notice how the body feels now versus five minutes ago — usually
  heavier, slower, warmer. Name what you find. That contrast IS the
  proof the technique worked.

Practice

Exercises (increasing difficulty)

Each has a hidden solution and the why. Do them roughly in order; don't rush to the later ones.

Exercise 1Warm-up

The 90-second stillness baseline. Lie in the projection-ready posture and stay completely physically still for 90 seconds. Count how many distinct urges to move you feel (itch, swallow, shift) and how many you successfully don't act on.

Success isn't zero urges — almost everyone gets several. What are you actually being graded on?
Treat each urge the way you treat a sensation in the body scan: notice it, name it ("itch, left calf"), and watch whether it fades if you don't service it.
Most beginners count 3–8 urges in 90 seconds and act on a couple. Success isn't zero urges — it's noticing each one and letting at least most of them pass un-serviced. Note your numbers in your journal; repeat until you can let all of them pass for 90 seconds. Why: this isolates the single hardest mechanical skill — not moving — and reframes the urge to move as just another sensation to notice → name → not react to. Itches and urges almost always fade in 20–40 seconds if you don't feed them, and proving that to yourself once changes the whole practice.
Exercise 2Warm-up

Belly-breath verification. Do Worked Example B, Phases 1–2, with both hands placed as described. Your task: get ten consecutive breaths where the belly hand clearly rises and the chest hand stays nearly still.

If the chest hand keeps rising, you're chest-breathing. Don't try to breathe bigger to fix it — try the opposite.
Lying down lets the diaphragm work with gravity; on the inhale, let the belly go slack and bulge rather than pulling the chest up.
If the chest hand keeps rising, you're chest-breathing. Fixes: (a) breathe smaller and lower, aiming the air "down past the belly button"; (b) on the inhale, consciously let the belly go slack and bulge rather than pulling the chest up; (c) try it lying down first, where the diaphragm works with gravity. Ten clean breaths = mechanics learned. Why: diaphragmatic breathing is what actually engages the slow, vagal, parasympathetic down-shift; chest breathing keeps arousal up. If the mechanics are wrong, every later breathing instruction quietly fails.
Exercise 3Core

Sensation-naming log. Run a 10-minute body scan (Worked Example A's scan portion, slowed down). Keep a notepad within reach. Afterward (not during), write down every distinct sensation you can recall and the plain label you gave it: e.g., "left hand — tingling," "feet — warm," "right forearm — faded/absent," "belly — neutral."

A good log spans several types of sensation, not just one. If you logged almost nothing, what might you have been doing to each region instead of feeling it?
A good log has 5–12 entries spanning several types (heaviness, tingling, warmth, fading, neutral). If you logged almost nothing, you were probably trying to relax each part instead of feeling it, or moving too fast — slow to ~30 seconds per region. If everything was "tense," you may be tensing-while-relaxing — add a slow PMR pass first. Why: naming sensations flatly is the core non-reactive-observer skill the whole course runs on; logging trains you to register them as neutral data instead of either ignoring them or reacting to them.
Exercise 4Core

The posture A/B test. On two different days, run the same 12-minute relaxation: once in your normal sleep position (sleep pillow, on your side, cozy), once in the projection-ready posture (supine, thin pillow, arms out, cooler, eyes softly open). Journal which one you stayed awake and present through, and which one you drifted/slept in.

There's an expected, instructive result here. If you slept through both, the problem isn't the posture — it's that your wakefulness anchors are too weak.
Nearly everyone stays more present in the projection-ready posture and drifts toward sleep in the sleep posture — that's the expected, instructive result. If you slept through both, your wakefulness anchors are too weak: raise the head more (semi-reclined chair), open the eyes, cool the room, practice when less sleep-deprived, and not right at bedtime. Why: it makes the posture's design rationale concrete and personal — you feel why each "uncozy" choice exists — and it surfaces, early, the falling-asleep problem that Module 02 will fully address.
Exercise 5Stretch

Non-reaction under a "trigger" sensation. During a scan, deliberately go looking for a fading or tingling limb. When you find one, hold attention on it for a full 60 seconds while applying notice → name → don't react → return. The instant you feel excitement ("is this it?!") or unease ("where's my arm?"), name that too — "excitement," "unease" — and return to neutral.

The grade isn't on the sensation — it's on your arousal level. What should stay flat the whole 60 seconds?
If you keep snapping back to alertness, the 60 seconds is too long for now. Shorten the interval and build it back up.
Success = you can rest attention on a strange sensation for 60 seconds and your arousal stays low — no urge to move the limb to "check," no emotional spike running away with you. If you keep snapping back to alertness, shorten to 20 seconds and build up. Why: excitement and fear are the two things that most reliably abort the deep states you'll build later (they spike sympathetic arousal and re-engage the body). Learning to meet a "weird" sensation with flat curiosity now, on something harmless, is exactly the emotional control that later keeps an experience from collapsing.
Exercise 6Challenge

The 15-minute hold. Put it all together: projection-ready posture → ~2 min settling breath → full PMR pass → body scan → rest. Stay physically still and mentally present for the whole 15 minutes (use a face-down timer). Journal: did you stay awake the whole time? How many movements did you make? What sensations showed up?

Don't expect 15 minutes on the first try. What's a realistic first milestone, and what counts as success even if you fall asleep?
If you fidgeted a lot, the bottleneck is stillness, not duration — go back and re-drill Exercise 1 before pushing the clock.

Exercise 7Challenge · refinement

"Hidden tension" sweep. At the end of any relaxation session, before you get up, do one slow extra pass looking only for places still secretly holding tension — the usual suspects: jaw, tongue, around the eyes, shoulders, hands, belly. For each one you find, take one slow exhale and let it drop a notch further.

Where is your tongue right now? Check the usual silent offenders before deciding you found "none."
Almost everyone finds residual tension somewhere — most often jaw, tongue (it's probably pressed to the roof of your mouth right now), or shoulders. Letting it go on an exhale deepens the state another level. If you find none, either you're very relaxed or not yet sensitive enough to detect it — re-do after a PMR pass, which makes hidden tension easier to spot by contrast. Why: "tensing while relaxing" is the most common silent failure mode; this sweep turns it into something you actively catch and correct, and it's the move that takes a session from "pretty relaxed" to "deeply relaxed."

Watch out

Common mistakes

⚠ Common mistakes

  • Tensing while "relaxing" — jaw clenched, tongue pressed up, shoulders hunched, toes curled, the whole session, unnoticed. Fix: always do a PMR pass first (the contrast reveals hidden tension), and end with the Exercise 7 hidden-tension sweep. Check jaw, tongue, eyes, shoulders specifically.
  • Falling asleep — you "come to" with the timer, no memory of the last stretch. Fix: this module, it's acceptable, even a good sign of deep relaxation. But to fix it: thinner/higher pillow or semi-reclined chair, eyes softly open, cooler room, lighter cover, and don't practice at bedtime or when sleep-deprived. (Staying awake at the edge is Module 02's core skill.)
  • Shallow chest breathing — chest hand rises, belly hand still; breath quick and high; you feel keyed-up, not calmer. Fix: re-train with Exercise 2 (two-hand check). Breathe smaller and lower. Make the exhale longer than the inhale — that's the part that actually calms.
  • Fidgeting / servicing every itch — constant micro-adjustments, scratching, shifting, deliberate swallowing. Fix: resolve to be still before you start; treat each urge as a sensation to notice-name-not-react-to. Most fade in 20–40 sec. Build with Exercise 1. (Real pain is the exception — adjust.)
  • Reacting to sensations — a tingle or fading limb triggers excitement ("is this it?!") or fear ("where's my arm?!"), which spikes arousal and pulls you back out. Fix: drill notice → name → don't react → return (Exercise 5). Name the emotion too ("excitement," "unease") and come back to neutral. Flat curiosity, never grabbing.
  • Over-controlling the breath — rigidly counting a strict pattern for the entire 20 minutes, keeping the mind too busy/alert to settle. Fix: use counted breathing only to settle in (first few minutes), then let the count dissolve into easy natural slow breathing.
  • Chasing the goal / forcing — straining to "feel something" or make a sensation happen, which is itself a form of tension. Fix: drop all agenda. Your only jobs here are relax and notice. The sensations come when you stop reaching for them.

Why: each "uncozy" choice is a small wakefulness anchor. A sleep posture is built to drop you unconscious; the projection-ready posture trades a little comfort to keep the mind present while the body relaxes. (Option a's "energy" mechanism is interpretive framing, not a claim this module makes.)

You're deep in a body scan and your left hand suddenly feels like it has vanished. The grounded explanation is that deep relaxation has quieted the touch/position signals the brain uses to map the body, so the part drops off the body-map — the so-called limb "_____" feeling. Why: it's the "fading" feeling. A part you can't feel clearly drops off the body-map — harmless and instantly reversible when you move. The correct response is notice → name it flatly ("left hand, faded") → don't react (don't move to check, don't get excited or alarmed) → return. Non-reaction is the whole point.

During a 4-7-8 breathing round, the 7-count hold makes you feel light-headed and slightly anxious. Best response?

Why: 4-7-8 is optional and only 3–4 rounds; if the hold causes light-headedness or anxiety, drop to gentle 4-out-6 belly breathing, which is just as valid for down-regulating arousal and never makes you dizzy. Forcing it (a, b) raises arousal — the opposite of the goal.

If you fall asleep during your relaxation practice in this module, you've failed the exercise.

Why: False. In Module 01, falling asleep means you relaxed thoroughly — half the skill is done. Note it in your journal, but it's not a failure here. Staying awake at the edge of sleep is the explicit job of Module 02; this module's job is just to relax the body deeply and notice it.

A sensation arises while you're settled. Put the four steps of the non-reactive observer drill in the correct order.

  • Return attention to the breath or the scan and let the sensation do whatever it does
  • Name it silently and flatly ("tingling in the left hand," "warmth")
  • Notice the sensation
  • Don't react — no checking the limb, no excitement, no alarm

Why: notice → name → don't react → return. Naming keeps you the calm observer; not reacting prevents the arousal spike that yanks you out of the settled state; returning lets the sensation pass on its own. This loop is the entire emotional skill of the practice.
