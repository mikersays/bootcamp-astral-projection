# Module 01 — The Foundation: Deep Relaxation & Body Awareness

**Rung:** Foundation
**Prerequisites:** `00-orientation`
**Promise:** Learn to relax the body completely while staying mentally present — the physical platform every other technique is built on.

> **How to read this module.** You have already (in Orientation) set up a practice space, started a journal, and read the safety boundaries. This module assumes nothing beyond that. We do **not** touch the threshold state, the vibrational state, or any exit technique here — those come in Modules 02–05. Your one job in this module is to build a body that will lie still, soft, and quiet on demand, and a mind that stays awake to watch it. Master this and everything later becomes *possible*. Skip it and everything later becomes *frustrating*.

---

## 1. Intro — Why relaxation is the platform

Every account of astral projection — Robert Monroe's, William Buhlman's, Robert Bruce's — starts in the same unglamorous place: a body so deeply relaxed it almost disappears, while the mind stays clear. The tradition has a tidy slogan for the goal of the *next* module, "mind awake, body asleep." You can't get there until the body part is reliable. That is what this module trains.

Here is the plain-language logic, no metaphysics required:

- **A tense body keeps the brain on alert.** Muscle tension, a racing heart, and quick shallow breathing are the body's "something is happening" signals. As long as they're firing, your nervous system stays in its activated, ready-to-move mode (the *sympathetic* "fight-or-flight" branch). You cannot drift toward the edge of sleep while your body is shouting *stay ready*.
- **Deep relaxation flips a real physiological switch.** When you systematically release muscle tension and slow your breathing — especially the out-breath — you shift the balance toward the *parasympathetic* "rest-and-digest" branch of the nervous system. Heart rate settles, arousal drops, attention softens. This is measurable, not mystical: progressive muscle relaxation produces steady decreases in skin electrical activity (a marker of parasympathetic activation) across a session, and slow breathing with a long exhale reliably raises heart-rate variability, a sign of stronger vagal (parasympathetic) tone.
- **A relaxed-but-awake body is the launchpad.** Once the body is deeply settled *and you are still consciously present*, you are in the doorway. Modules 02+ teach you how to stand in that doorway without falling through it into ordinary sleep. But the doorway only exists once the body is relaxed enough. No platform, no projection.

**The three lenses, kept separate** (a habit this whole course holds to):

| Lens | What it covers in this module |
|---|---|
| **Experiential** (reported, real-to-you, not externally verified) | Heaviness, tingling, warmth, the felt "fading" or disappearance of a limb, the body feeling huge/tiny/numb. Practitioners report these constantly. You will likely feel them. |
| **Evidenced** (measurable, studied) | Parasympathetic down-regulation from PMR and slow breathing; the body-scan/interoception research base; respiratory sinus arrhythmia (heart slows on the exhale). |
| **Interpretive** (the tradition's framing) | That these sensations are "energy," the loosening of the astral body, or pre-separation signs. We will *name* this framing when it's relevant, but in this module we simply observe the sensations and do not assert any cause. |

**A grounded reassurance you can lean on now.** Nothing in this module asks you to leave anything. You are learning to relax and to pay attention. The strange sensations you'll meet (a limb "vanishing," the body feeling like it's tilting) are well-known products of deep relaxation and the brain's body-map quieting down — harmless, reversible the instant you move, and exactly the raw material you'll work with later. If at any point you want out, you open your eyes, wiggle your fingers and toes, and you're fully back. That escape hatch never closes.

**Safety, woven in (not bolted on).** Relaxation practice is gentle and broadly safe. Three honest notes:
- If Orientation flagged you (history of psychosis or schizophrenia-spectrum conditions, dissociative disorders, severe anxiety/panic), the guidance there still holds — relaxation and body scanning are usually fine and even helpful, but check with a professional before *intensive* practice, and stop if a session reliably increases distress or a feeling of unreality.
- A small number of people feel *more* anxious when they turn attention inward or try to relax ("relaxation-induced anxiety"). If that's you, keep sessions short, keep your eyes open or softly open, and favor the breathing and tension-release drills over long silent body scans. It usually eases with practice; if it doesn't, that's worth raising with a professional.
- This is not medical or psychological treatment and makes no health claims. It's a skill-building practice.

## 2. Lessons

We build one tool at a time, then stack them. Read all six; the worked scripts in Section 3 combine them.

### Lesson 2.1 — Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR): tension, then release

**The idea.** Developed by physician Edmund Jacobson (who published *Progressive Relaxation* in 1938), PMR works by a simple trick of contrast: you **deliberately tense** a muscle group for a few seconds, then **let it go all at once** and notice the release. Two things make this powerful for a beginner:

1. **Contrast teaches your nervous system what "released" actually feels like.** Most people walk around with low-grade tension they can't even detect. Tensing first makes the *letting-go* unmistakable — you can't miss the difference.
2. **The release itself down-regulates arousal.** Across a PMR session, parasympathetic activity rises progressively (measurable as falling skin-conductance), which is precisely the settling you want.

PMR is also one of the better-evidenced relaxation tools generally — it was rated an effective non-drug treatment for chronic insomnia by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine, which is relevant here because *falling asleep too fast* is the beginner's main enemy, and a body this relaxed is one you'll later have to learn to keep on the *edge* of sleep rather than over it.

**The exact tension-and-release rule (memorize this):**
- Tense the target group **firmly but never to the point of pain or cramp** — about 70% effort, not a maximal strain. Joints and old injuries: go light or skip.
- Hold the tension for **about 5 seconds** while you keep breathing (don't hold your breath).
- **Release completely and instantly** — don't ease off gradually, *drop* it — and let that group go limp.
- Rest and **notice the contrast for about 10–15 seconds** before moving on. This noticing is the actual training, not the tensing.

**The exact muscle-group sequence (work it head-to-toe so the deepest relaxation pools where you'll rest last — feet up is also fine and traditional; pick one and be consistent).** Here is the standard head-to-toe order, condensed to a beginner-friendly set of groups:

1. **Forehead & scalp** — raise your eyebrows high, wrinkle the forehead. Release.
2. **Eyes & cheeks** — squeeze your eyes shut tight and scrunch the nose. Release.
3. **Jaw & mouth** — clench the jaw and press the lips together (don't grind teeth). Release; let the jaw hang slightly open.
4. **Neck** — *gently* press the back of your head down into the surface, or lightly tip the chin toward the chest. Go easy here. Release.
5. **Shoulders** — shrug them up toward your ears. Release; let them melt down and back.
6. **Upper arms** — bend the elbows and tense the biceps. Release.
7. **Forearms & hands** — make tight fists and tense down to the wrists. Release; let the fingers half-curl open.
8. **Chest & upper back** — take a fuller breath and squeeze the chest/shoulder blades gently together. Release as you exhale.
9. **Abdomen** — tighten the belly as if bracing for a light poke. Release.
10. **Buttocks & hips** — squeeze the glutes together. Release.
11. **Thighs** — press the thighs down / tense the quads. Release.
12. **Calves** — *gently* point the toes toward your face (flex the ankle) to tense the calves. (Pointing toes *away* can cramp — flexing toward you is safer.) Release.
13. **Feet & toes** — curl the toes downward lightly. Release.

End by lying still and letting the whole body be heavy for a minute. That's one full PMR pass — roughly 8–12 minutes at an unhurried pace.

> **Common beginner error, flagged now:** *tensing while you think you're relaxing.* People clench the jaw, hunch the shoulders, or curl the toes the whole session without noticing. PMR's tense-then-release contrast is the antidote — and in Section 5 we'll add a "scan for hidden tension" habit.

### Lesson 2.2 — The point-by-point body scan

**The idea.** Where PMR *does* something to each muscle, the body scan does the opposite: you **just pay attention** to each part of the body in turn, with no agenda to change it. You move a spotlight of attention slowly from point to point, noticing whatever sensation is there — pressure, warmth, tingling, weight, or nothing at all — and then moving on.

This is the single most important skill in the whole module, because **noticing body sensation without reacting is the core skill of every later stage.** The vibrational state (Module 03) and the exit cues (Module 05) are all *sensations you observe without grabbing*. The body scan is where you build that muscle.

It's also well-studied: the body scan sits at the heart of Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR), one of the most-researched behavioral interventions there is, and regular practice measurably improves **interoception** — your accuracy at reading internal body signals — and reduces the tendency to catastrophically misread normal sensations. That last point matters: you're literally training the skill of feeling a weird sensation and *not* panicking about it.

**The route (classic MBSR order, lying down):** start at the **toes of one foot** → sole, top, and heel of that foot → ankle → lower leg → knee → thigh → then **cross over** and do the other leg the same way, foot up to thigh → **pelvis and hips** → lower back → abdomen → chest → upper back → **shoulders** → upper arms → forearms → hands and fingers (one arm then the other, or both together) → **neck and throat** → jaw and face → scalp → and finally **rest in awareness of the whole body at once** as a single field.

**How to actually do it:**
- Spend roughly **20–40 seconds per region**, longer if it's interesting, shorter if attention won't settle. A full scan runs ~10–20 minutes.
- **Don't try to relax the part — just feel it.** Paradoxically, attention-without-agenda is what lets a part release. If it doesn't release, that's fine; note "tense" and move on.
- When you find **no sensation** in a region, that *is* the observation. Note "blank" or "neutral" and move on. (That neutrality is often the doorway to the "fading" feeling in Lesson 2.4.)
- When the mind wanders (it will, constantly), note where it went and gently bring the spotlight back to where you left off. The returning *is* the practice; you're not failing when you wander.

### Lesson 2.3 — Slow diaphragmatic breathing

**The idea.** The breath is the one autonomic process you can steer by hand, and steering it slow — especially a **long exhale** — is the fastest reliable way to down-regulate arousal.

**Get the mechanics right first (diaphragmatic = "belly" breathing):**
- Lie down, one hand on your chest, one on your belly.
- Breathe in *through the nose* so that the **belly hand rises** and the **chest hand stays nearly still.** You're letting the diaphragm drop and the belly balloon out. Shallow chest breathing (chest hand rising, belly still) is the thing to *unlearn* — it keeps arousal up.
- The out-breath is passive and slow — let the belly fall.

**Why the long exhale specifically calms you (the evidenced mechanism).** Your heart rate naturally speeds slightly on the inhale and slows on the exhale — this is *respiratory sinus arrhythmia*. On the exhale, the vagus nerve releases acetylcholine and slows the heart; a *longer* exhale stretches out that parasympathetic, heart-slowing phase. So making the exhale longer than the inhale acutely raises vagal (parasympathetic) tone. That's the whole reason the counts below are weighted toward the out-breath.

**Two concrete patterns — pick by feel:**

**(A) Simple slow belly breathing (start here).** No breath-holding, gentle, sustainable for the whole session:
- **In through the nose for a count of 4**, belly rising.
- **Out through the nose (or pursed lips) for a count of 6**, belly falling, slow and smooth.
- That's a ~10-second cycle, ~6 breaths per minute, which is near the rate where heart-rate variability and the relaxation response peak. Just keep this rhythm soft and even. This is your default for long sessions because it never makes you light-headed.

**(B) The 4-7-8 breath (a stronger, shorter down-shift).** Popularized as a relaxation/sleep tool; a scoping review across 15 studies links it to reduced acute stress and increased heart-rate variability via vagal pathways (note: promising but still needing more independent replication — that's the honest evidence status). The pattern, done a tongue-on-the-ridge-behind-the-upper-teeth way in the original:
- **Inhale quietly through the nose for 4.**
- **Hold for 7.**
- **Exhale through the mouth, slowly, for 8** (the long, weighted exhale is the active ingredient).
- Do **only 3–4 cycles**, then return to normal breathing. The 7-count hold can make beginners light-headed or, for a few people, slightly anxious — if so, drop straight back to pattern (A). Don't grind out many rounds.

**Important transition note for this practice specifically:** you'll use breathing to *settle in* at the start of a session, but you won't white-knuckle a strict count for 20 minutes. After the first few minutes you let the count dissolve into easy, slow, natural belly breathing so it doesn't keep your mind too busy to settle. (Why this matters becomes obvious in Module 02, where an over-controlled mind won't drift to the threshold.)

### Lesson 2.4 — Noticing and naming subtle sensations, without reacting

As the body settles, it starts to *report things*. The classic menu:
- **Heaviness** — limbs feel weighted, sinking, hard to move (often the first sign you're going deep).
- **Tingling / pins-and-needles / a fine buzzing** — especially in hands, feet, lips, scalp.
- **Warmth** (sometimes coolness) — spreading through the hands, belly, or whole body.
- **Limb "fading"** — you lose the sense of where your hand or foot *is*; it feels absent, dissolved, or like it's not yours. The body can feel oddly large, small, tilted, or like it's gently floating or sinking.

**The grounded view (so these never scare you):** as deep relaxation quiets the steady stream of touch and position signals the brain normally uses to map the body, the map gets fuzzy. A part you're not actively moving and can no longer feel clearly simply drops off the map — hence "fading." None of it means anything is wrong; it means relaxation is working and your body-map is going quiet. The tradition will later read some of these as pre-separation signs; for now we make **no** claim about cause — we just notice.

**The one skill to drill: notice → name → don't react → return.**
1. **Notice** the sensation.
2. **Name** it, silently and flatly: *"heaviness," "tingling in the left hand," "warmth," "the foot's gone fuzzy."* Naming keeps you the calm observer instead of getting swept into the feeling.
3. **Don't react.** Don't move to check the limb is still there. Don't get excited ("is this it?!"). Don't get scared ("why can't I feel my arm?"). Excitement and fear both spike arousal and yank you back out of the settled state — this is *the* lesson that pays off hugest later.
4. **Return** your attention to the breath or the scan and let the sensation do whatever it does.

That neutral, slightly-curious, non-grabbing stance is the entire emotional skill of this practice. Practice it on a tingle now; you'll need it on much stranger things later.

### Lesson 2.5 — The projection-ready posture

This is **not** your normal sleeping position, and the difference is deliberate. A sleep posture is engineered to drop you *unconscious*. The projection-ready posture is engineered to let the **body** go fully relaxed while keeping a **thread of consciousness alive** — relaxed body, awake mind. (You won't *use* that awake-mind balance until Module 02; here you're just building the posture so it becomes automatic.)

**The default: supine and neutral.**
- **On your back (supine),** lying on a firm-ish surface — a bed is okay but a too-soft mattress that swallows you invites sleep. A yoga mat with a thin pad, or a firm couch, works well.
- **Spine neutral and straight**, legs uncrossed and slightly apart (about hip width) so the hips fall open and nothing presses limb-on-limb.
- **Arms at your sides, slightly away from the torso**, palms down or up — whatever lets the shoulders drop. *Not* folded on the chest (restricts the belly breath) and *not* interlaced (limbs touching keeps the body-map "louder," which fights the fading you want).
- **Head:** here's the key anti-sleep tweak. Use a **thin pillow or a slight elevation** so the head and neck are supported and the chin is very slightly tucked — comfortable, but **not** the deep, fully-supported sink of your sleeping pillow. Some practitioners deliberately keep the head a touch *more* upright than for sleep (a low wedge, or practicing semi-reclined in a supported chair at roughly a 40-degree angle) precisely because lying flat the way you sleep is the fast track to unconsciousness.
- **Eyes closed**, or — if you fall asleep easily — softly *open* or half-open, gaze unfocused toward the ceiling. Open eyes is a legitimate, traditional anti-sleep trick.
- **Cool rather than warm, lightly covered rather than cozy.** Warmth and heavy blankets are sleep cues. A slightly cool room with a light cover keeps you on the awake side.

**Why it differs from a sleep posture — the principle:** every choice trades a little bit of "cozy" for a little bit of "stay aware." Firmer surface, thinner pillow, cooler room, head slightly raised, arms not tucked in — each is a small wakefulness anchor. You want the *body* to believe it's bedtime while your *mind* keeps one foot in the room. That's the whole craft of the posture.

**Contraindication note:** if lying flat on your back is uncomfortable for you (pregnancy in later stages, certain back conditions, reflux), use the semi-reclined supported-chair version instead — it serves the same purpose and is actually *better* at preventing sleep.

### Lesson 2.6 — Holding it for 15–20 minutes

The objective is to lie in the projection-ready posture, deeply relaxed, **physically still** and mentally present, for **15–20 minutes** at a stretch. Two sub-skills:

**Physical stillness on demand.** Once you've settled, you stop *all* voluntary movement — no scratching the itch, no shifting the shoulder, no swallowing-on-purpose. This matters because every fidget reloads the body-map (the opposite of fading) and re-engages the alert, ready-to-move system. The trick: **resolve, before you start, to be still, and treat every urge to move as just another sensation to notice → name → not react to → let pass.** Almost every itch and urge fades within 20–40 seconds if you don't service it. (Genuine pain or pins-and-needles that hurts is the exception — adjust, don't suffer.)

**Staying present for the duration.** Hold an easy thread of awareness — the slow breath, or a slow repeating scan — so you neither drop into sleep nor get bored and quit. Build the duration gradually (see exercises): 5 minutes solidly still and present is a real win before you chase 20.

**A note you'll appreciate later:** in this module, *if you do fall asleep, that's not a disaster* — it means you relaxed thoroughly, which is half the skill. The other half (staying awake at the edge) is literally Module 02's entire job. So train relaxation hard here; we'll add the awake-at-the-edge control next.

## 3. Worked examples (full followable scripts)

These are written out so you can read them slowly into a voice memo and play them back, or have someone read them to you, or just internalize the arc. Counts are in seconds. *(Optional but recommended: record Script A in your own calm voice with long pauses — it's the single best way to learn the sequence.)*

### Worked Example A — The full ~15-minute "PMR-then-Scan" session

*Setup:* Projection-ready posture (Lesson 2.5). Phone on Do-Not-Disturb, a soft timer set for 16 minutes face-down so you can't clock-watch.

> **[0:00 — Arrival, ~1 min]**
> Settle onto your back. Legs hip-width, arms a little away from your sides, palms up. Let the surface take your full weight. Close your eyes, or leave them softly open toward the ceiling. Make a quiet resolution: *for the next fifteen minutes I will lie still, and I will stay here watching.*
>
> **[~1:00 — Settling breath, ~2 min]**
> Bring one hand's worth of attention to your belly. Breathe in through the nose for a slow count of **four** — feel the belly rise. Out through the nose for a slow count of **six** — feel the belly fall. *(In 2-3-4… out 2-3-4-5-6…)* Again. And again — about a dozen of these slow breaths. Let each exhale be the longer, heavier one; let it carry a little tension out of you each time. After a dozen, stop counting and just let the breath run slow and easy on its own.
>
> **[~3:00 — PMR pass, ~7 min]**
> Now we tense and release, head to toe. Tense about seventy percent — never to pain. Hold five, then drop it all at once and feel the difference for ten.
> Forehead and scalp — lift the eyebrows high… hold, two, three, four, five… and *release.* Feel it smooth out.
> Eyes and cheeks — squeeze them shut, scrunch the nose… hold… and *release.*
> Jaw — clench gently, lips together… hold… and *release;* let the jaw hang loose.
> Neck — press the head lightly back, go easy… hold… and *release.*
> Shoulders — shrug them up to your ears… hold… and *release;* let them melt down.
> Upper arms — bend the elbows, tense the biceps… hold… *release.*
> Hands and forearms — tight fists… hold… *release;* fingers fall open.
> Chest and upper back — a fuller breath, squeeze the shoulder blades gently… hold… *release* on the out-breath.
> Belly — tighten as if braced… hold… *release.*
> Buttocks and hips — squeeze… hold… *release.*
> Thighs — press them down… hold… *release.*
> Calves — flex the toes up toward your face, gently… hold… *release.*
> Feet and toes — curl the toes down lightly… hold… *release.*
> Now lie completely still and let the whole body be heavy, sinking, for a slow count of fifteen.
>
> **[~10:00 — Body scan, ~5 min]**
> Now we only watch — change nothing. Bring the spotlight of attention to the **toes of your left foot.** Just feel whatever's there — warmth, tingling, pressure, or nothing. Name it quietly: *"tingling," "warm," "blank."* … Move to the **sole and top of the foot** … the **ankle** … **lower leg** … **knee** … **thigh.** … Cross to the **right toes** … **foot** … **ankle** … **lower leg** … **knee** … **thigh.** … Up into the **hips and pelvis,** let them be heavy … **lower back** … **belly,** rising and falling … **chest** … **upper back** … out to the **shoulders,** soft and dropped … down the **arms** … into the **hands and fingers** — they may tingle or feel far away; just name it … **neck and throat** … **jaw and face,** soft … **scalp.** … Now let the whole body be one single field of sensation at once. Heavy. Quiet. Present.
>
> **[~15:00 — Rest, until the timer]**
> Stay here. Slow breath, still body. If a limb has gone fuzzy or vanished, just name it — *"the left hand's faded"* — and let it be. You're not going anywhere; you're just resting awake in a very relaxed body. When the timer sounds: wiggle fingers and toes, take a fuller breath, stretch, and come all the way back before you stand.

### Worked Example B — The 5-minute breathing drill walk-through

*Goal:* train the diaphragmatic mechanics and the calming long exhale on their own, so they're automatic when you stack them. Do this seated or supine.

> **Phase 1 — Find the belly breath (~1 min).** One hand on the chest, one on the belly. Breathe normally and just *watch which hand moves.* If it's the chest hand, slow down and consciously send the next breath low — aim it at the belly hand. Keep going until the belly hand is the one rising and the chest hand is nearly still. Don't force a big breath; a relaxed medium breath that goes *low* is the target.
>
> **Phase 2 — Slow 4-out-6 (~2.5 min, ~15 breaths).** Now add the count. In through the nose, belly rising, **2-3-4.** Out, belly falling, slow and smooth, **2-3-4-5-6.** Keep the exhale longer and softer than the inhale — that long exhale is the part that slows your heart. Find an even, unhurried rhythm. If you get light-headed, the breaths are too big — make them smaller and slower, not deeper.
>
> **Phase 3 — Three 4-7-8 cycles (optional, ~1 min).** Rest the tongue lightly behind your upper front teeth. Inhale through the nose for **4.** Hold for **7** (stay relaxed; don't strain). Exhale slowly through the mouth for **8.** That's one cycle. Do **three more,** no more. If the 7-hold makes you tense or dizzy, skip this phase entirely and stay with 4-out-6 — it's just as valid.
>
> **Phase 4 — Let go of the count (~30 sec).** Stop counting. Let the breath run slow and natural on its own and notice how the body feels now versus five minutes ago — usually heavier, slower, warmer. Name what you find. That contrast *is* the proof the technique worked.

## 4. Exercises (increasing difficulty)

Each has a hidden solution and the *why.* Do them roughly in order; don't rush to the later ones.

**Exercise 1 — The 90-second stillness baseline (easy).**
Lie in the projection-ready posture and stay *completely* physically still for 90 seconds. Count how many distinct urges to move you feel (itch, swallow, shift) and how many you successfully *don't* act on.

<details><summary><b>Solution & why</b></summary>

**Solution:** Most beginners count 3–8 urges in 90 seconds and act on a couple. Success isn't zero urges — it's *noticing* each one and letting at least most of them pass un-serviced. Note your numbers in your journal; repeat until you can let *all* of them pass for 90 seconds. **Why:** this isolates the single hardest mechanical skill — not moving — and reframes the urge to move as just another sensation to notice-name-not-react. Itches and urges almost always fade in 20–40 seconds if you don't feed them, and proving that to yourself once changes the whole practice.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — Belly-breath verification (easy).**
Do Worked Example B, Phases 1–2, with both hands placed as described. Your task: get **ten consecutive breaths** where the belly hand clearly rises and the chest hand stays nearly still.

<details><summary><b>Solution & why</b></summary>

**Solution:** If the chest hand keeps rising, you're chest-breathing. Fixes: (a) breathe *smaller and lower*, aiming the air "down past the belly button"; (b) on the inhale, consciously let the belly go slack and bulge rather than pulling the chest up; (c) try it lying down first, where the diaphragm works with gravity. Ten clean breaths = mechanics learned. **Why:** diaphragmatic breathing is what actually engages the slow, vagal, parasympathetic down-shift; chest breathing keeps arousal *up*. If the mechanics are wrong, every later breathing instruction quietly fails.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — Sensation-naming log (medium).**
Run a 10-minute body scan (Worked Example A's scan portion, slowed down). Keep a notepad within reach. *Afterward* (not during), write down every distinct sensation you can recall and the plain label you gave it: e.g., "left hand — tingling," "feet — warm," "right forearm — faded/absent," "belly — neutral."

<details><summary><b>Solution & why</b></summary>

**Solution:** A good log has 5–12 entries spanning several *types* (heaviness, tingling, warmth, fading, neutral). If you logged almost nothing, you were probably trying to *relax* each part instead of *feeling* it, or moving too fast — slow to ~30 seconds per region. If everything was "tense," you may be tensing-while-relaxing (see Section 5) — add a slow PMR pass first. **Why:** naming sensations flatly is the core non-reactive-observer skill the whole course runs on; logging trains you to register them as neutral data instead of either ignoring them or reacting to them.
</details>

**Exercise 4 — The posture A/B test (medium).**
On two different days, run the same 12-minute relaxation: once in your **normal sleep position** (sleep pillow, on your side, cozy), once in the **projection-ready posture** (supine, thin pillow, arms out, cooler, eyes softly open). Journal which one you stayed *awake and present* through, and which one you drifted/slept in.

<details><summary><b>Solution & why</b></summary>

**Solution:** Nearly everyone stays more present in the projection-ready posture and drifts toward sleep in the sleep posture — that's the expected, instructive result. If you slept through *both,* your wakefulness anchors are too weak: raise the head more (semi-reclined chair), open the eyes, cool the room, practice when less sleep-deprived, and *not* right at bedtime. **Why:** it makes the posture's design rationale concrete and personal — you *feel* why each "uncozy" choice exists — and it surfaces, early, the falling-asleep problem that Module 02 will fully address.
</details>

**Exercise 5 — Non-reaction under a "trigger" sensation (medium-hard).**
During a scan, *deliberately* go looking for a fading or tingling limb. When you find one, hold attention on it for a full 60 seconds while applying notice → name → don't react → return. The instant you feel excitement ("is this it?!") or unease ("where's my arm?"), name *that* too — "excitement," "unease" — and return to neutral.

<details><summary><b>Solution & why</b></summary>

**Solution:** Success = you can rest attention on a strange sensation for 60 seconds and your arousal *stays low* — no urge to move the limb to "check," no emotional spike running away with you. If you keep snapping back to alertness, shorten to 20 seconds and build up. **Why:** excitement and fear are the two things that most reliably abort the deep states you'll build later (they spike sympathetic arousal and re-engage the body). Learning to meet a "weird" sensation with flat curiosity *now*, on something harmless, is exactly the emotional control that later keeps an experience from collapsing.
</details>

**Exercise 6 — The 15-minute hold (hard).**
Put it all together: projection-ready posture → ~2 min settling breath → full PMR pass → body scan → rest. Stay physically still and mentally present for the **whole 15 minutes** (use a face-down timer). Journal: did you stay awake the whole time? How many movements did you make? What sensations showed up?

<details><summary><b>Solution & why</b></summary>

**Solution:** The target is 15 minutes, still and awake, with near-zero deliberate movements. Realistic progression: most people first manage ~5 minutes solidly, then build. If you fell asleep, that's *fine for this module* (it proves you relaxed deeply) — but note it, because staying awake is the next skill. If you fidgeted a lot, revisit Exercise 1. **Why:** 15–20 minutes still, relaxed, and present *is* the module's headline objective — the physical platform every later technique launches from. This exercise is the platform itself.
</details>

**Exercise 7 — "Hidden tension" sweep (hard, refinement).**
At the end of any relaxation session, before you get up, do one slow extra pass looking *only* for places still secretly holding tension — the usual suspects: jaw, tongue, around the eyes, shoulders, hands, belly. For each one you find, take one slow exhale and let it drop a notch further.

<details><summary><b>Solution & why</b></summary>

**Solution:** Almost everyone finds residual tension somewhere — most often jaw, tongue (it's probably pressed to the roof of your mouth right now), or shoulders. Letting it go on an exhale deepens the state another level. If you find *none,* either you're very relaxed or not yet sensitive enough to detect it — re-do after a PMR pass, which makes hidden tension easier to spot by contrast. **Why:** "tensing while relaxing" is the most common silent failure mode; this sweep turns it into something you actively catch and correct, and it's the move that takes a session from "pretty relaxed" to "deeply relaxed."
</details>

## 5. Common mistakes

| Mistake | What it looks like | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Tensing while "relaxing"** | Jaw clenched, tongue pressed up, shoulders hunched, toes curled — the whole session, unnoticed. | Always do a PMR pass first (the contrast reveals hidden tension), and end with the Exercise 7 hidden-tension sweep. Check jaw, tongue, eyes, shoulders specifically. |
| **Falling asleep** | You "come to" with the timer, no memory of the last stretch. | This module: acceptable, even a good sign of deep relaxation. But to fix it: thinner/higher pillow or semi-reclined chair, eyes softly open, cooler room, lighter cover, and don't practice at bedtime or when sleep-deprived. (Staying awake at the edge is Module 02's core skill.) |
| **Shallow chest breathing** | Chest hand rises, belly hand still; breath quick and high; you feel keyed-up, not calmer. | Re-train with Exercise 2 (two-hand check). Breathe *smaller and lower.* Make the exhale longer than the inhale — that's the part that actually calms. |
| **Fidgeting / servicing every itch** | Constant micro-adjustments, scratching, shifting, deliberate swallowing. | Resolve to be still before you start; treat each urge as a sensation to notice-name-not-react-to. Most fade in 20–40 sec. Build with Exercise 1. (Real pain is the exception — adjust.) |
| **Reacting to sensations** | A tingle or fading limb triggers excitement ("is this it?!") or fear ("where's my arm?!"), which spikes arousal and pulls you back out. | Drill notice → name → don't react → return (Exercise 5). Name the *emotion* too ("excitement," "unease") and come back to neutral. Flat curiosity, never grabbing. |
| **Over-controlling the breath** | Rigidly counting a strict pattern for the entire 20 minutes, keeping the mind too busy/alert to settle. | Use counted breathing only to *settle in* (first few minutes), then let the count dissolve into easy natural slow breathing. |
| **Chasing the goal / forcing** | Straining to "feel something" or make a sensation happen, which is itself a form of tension. | Drop all agenda. Your only jobs here are *relax* and *notice.* The sensations come when you stop reaching for them. |

## 6. Checkpoint quiz

**Q1 (multiple choice).** In progressive muscle relaxation, after you tense a muscle group, you should:
a) Ease off the tension slowly over several seconds
b) Release it completely and instantly, then notice the contrast
c) Hold the tension until it's uncomfortable, then move on
d) Tense the next group while keeping this one tense

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**(b).** The instant, complete release is what teaches your nervous system the felt difference between tense and relaxed — that contrast *is* the technique, and noticing it is the actual training.
</details>

**Q2 (true/false).** Making your exhale longer than your inhale is just a comfort preference with no real physiological effect.

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** The heart slows on the exhale (respiratory sinus arrhythmia, driven by the vagus nerve); a longer exhale extends that parasympathetic, heart-slowing phase and measurably raises vagal tone. The weighting toward the out-breath is the active ingredient, not a preference.
</details>

**Q3 (multiple choice).** Why does the projection-ready posture use a thinner pillow, cooler room, and arms held away from the body instead of your cozy sleep setup?
a) Tradition requires it for the energy to flow
b) To relax the body while keeping a thread of wakefulness, so you don't simply fall asleep
c) It makes you fall asleep faster
d) It has no real purpose; any comfortable position works

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**(b).** Each "uncozy" choice is a small wakefulness anchor. A sleep posture is built to drop you unconscious; the projection-ready posture trades a little comfort to keep the mind present while the body relaxes. (Note: we don't assert any "energy" mechanism — that's option (a)'s interpretive framing, not a claim this module makes.)
</details>

**Q4 (short answer).** You're deep in a body scan and your left hand suddenly feels like it has vanished — you can't sense where it is. What's the grounded explanation, and what exactly do you do?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Grounded explanation:** deep relaxation has quieted the touch/position signals your brain uses to map the body, so a part you can't feel clearly drops off the body-map — the "fading" feeling. It's harmless and reverses the instant you move. **What you do:** notice → name it flatly ("left hand, faded") → *don't react* (don't move to check, don't get excited or alarmed) → return attention to the scan/breath. Non-reaction is the whole point.
</details>

**Q5 (multiple choice).** During a 4-7-8 breathing round, the 7-count hold makes you feel light-headed and slightly anxious. Best response?
a) Push through several more rounds to build tolerance
b) Hold your breath even longer to "break through"
c) Stop the 4-7-8 and return to simple slow 4-in / 6-out belly breathing
d) Abandon breathing practice entirely

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**(c).** 4-7-8 is optional and only 3–4 rounds; if the hold causes light-headedness or anxiety, drop to gentle 4-out-6 belly breathing, which is just as valid for down-regulating arousal and never makes you dizzy. Forcing it (a, b) raises arousal — the opposite of the goal.
</details>

**Q6 (true/false).** If you fall asleep during your relaxation practice in *this* module, you've failed the exercise.

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** In Module 01, falling asleep means you relaxed thoroughly — half the skill is done. Note it in your journal, but it's not a failure here. Staying awake *at the edge* of sleep is the explicit job of Module 02; this module's job is just to relax the body deeply and notice it.
</details>
